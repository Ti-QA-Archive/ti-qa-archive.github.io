    • Muhammad's assessment is pretty good. As stated, the size and memory consumption difference is due t the v8 engine being packages with your android app. This is what allows you to use Javascript in the fastest way possible in your app.

      You probably aren't going to see the big advantages of Alloy by basing your assessment on a small, basic app. Alloy's strengths come in its organization, conventions, and structure that allow you to scale and maintain apps much more easily than you would without a framework. That's not to say these things can't be achieved in Android native development, but with Alloy, Titanium's best practices have been encapsulated fo you.

      Despite being the lead developer of Alloy and a former evangelist here at Appcelerator, I would still never tell you what framework or platform is right for you without knowing your specific goals. If the information you've given here is the extent of it, it sounds like you want to create a basic app and have already done so in native Android development. If you don't need to take it any further action, then Alloy doesn't really sound like a fit for you. That said, if your app is meant to be, or inadvertently grows to be, more complex, I think advantages of Alloy will quickly becomes self-evident.

      One picky side note: I don't personally find Android notation to be less complex than Alloy or even plain old Titanium in many cases. Or perhaps even the most basic case of accessing an element from a view.

      android

      (TypeCast)findViewById(R.id.someElementId);

      alloy

      $.someElementId

      That's obviously an oversimplification, but you can see where Alloy and its use of Javascript often strips away seemingly unneeded levels of complexity for simple behaviors.

  • Hi Aniruddha,

    Refer this link : Why Alloy is important?.

    I think your app is definitely appropriate for alloy, the same things appropriate for Titanium in general work with Alloy. One of the key things to think about is that using Alloy will not necessarily speed up development time. That is not the goal, the goal is to separate the concerns (Model View Controller), and provide cross-platform and multiple form factor support. If you are interested purely in speed of development stick with regular Titanium.

    However, where it can speed up dev time is in your persistence strategy, sql integration of models is built in with Alloy. So, if this flashcard app your thinking of creating has the users creating flashcards on their phone, then using them later, I would definitely go with Alloy to take advantage of the model stuff.

    Take note that the documentation is kind of sparse, I ported an existing clients project to Alloy and really had to find my own way on a lot of things, but It was not a simple app.

    Essentially, if you want to :

    • Release to multiple platforms and form factors and not have migraines,
    • Have a built-in easy to use persistence strategy,
    • Be able to maintain your code at a later date, or have other people look at it and be able to tell whats going on, then I would definitely use Alloy.
