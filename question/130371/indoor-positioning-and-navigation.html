  • Matias, in general, you cannot get "fine" location data from inside a building, because your phone can't "see" the GPS satellites. However, there is such a thing as "Differential GPS", which uses ground-based transmitters to supplement the satellite-based transmitters, but that is a little off-topic.

    I looked at the American Museum of Natural History app's demo video. They talk about "turn-by-turn" directions, but they show the user pressing a button at each turn. You will also notice that the GPS indicator is not lit up on the demo device, even when they talk about "pinpointing" your current location in the museum. This app is not tracking a "fine" (GPS-based) location.

    They have wifi all over that museum. What I think is going on here is that phone will attach to whatever wifi is strongest, and the individual access points in that museum are in known locations, like major hallway intersections. The app is reading which access point is being used, and assuming an approximate location from that. You can see that the blue halo of approximate position is large in the demo video.

  • Which platforms are you developing this for?

    iOS is a bit tricky, as you aren't allowed to publish apps that make use of wifi signal strength measurements (which is a common way to do indoor positioning on the device). As Cory mentioned, you could also do the positioning calculation on the infrastructure hardware. I think Meridian used some specialized Cisco routers for their system in the Museum of Natural History.

    For Android, there are a few options for radio-based indoor positioning. I've heard good things about the indoor positioning API from Qubulus. Eventually, Google may open up their indoor mapping & positioning API, but it's still early.
