Why do a Titanium App look Android 2.2 on an Android 4.0?

Hi,

I've been working on comparison project comparing PhoneGap/jQuery Mobile vs Appcelerator Titanium.

From the start I've been very excited about the speed of Titanium compared to the other… And though I experienced in stability and crashes I had high hope that Titanium was the answer for me to build a nice native UX guideline true apps for both iOS and Android in one process..

I started of building for Android 2.3 because that is what I had… But now my wife got an Android 4.0. Running my app on her phone I was disappointed that native UI elements doesn't look at all Android 4.0… For instance the modal date picker has Android 2.2 buttons (though) they have a darker color….

I thought the hole idea of Titanium compared to the other is that it uses native UI elements!?

So why doesn't I get a native Android 4.0 modal date picker running the app on a Android 4.0???

Also where are all the new fancy Android 3.0+ UI elements like an Action Bar? Searching the web I find that it's being considered for Titanium 2.2…. It was presented on Google I/O 2010!!!

Is the focus of Appcelerator mainly iOS? Look for Android 4.0 widgets for Appcelerator I stumble upon http://www.androiduipatterns.com/2012/03/multi-platform-frameworks-destroy.html . Apparently other share by disappointment with Titanium…

Apparently all Titanium Android apps are reimplemented with real native Android api… Can any share links for a Titanium Android apps which do follow the native UX guidelines and is good enough for the developer to wanting to share them on Google Play???

/Christian

    It's because you didn't read Google's docs on android development. :) uses-sdk

    In order to get newer UI elements, you have to change your manifest so Android knows that's what you want to do.

      Thank you for your pointers!!

      For others to follow I added the following to the <android> section of tiapp.xml:

      <manifest>
    <uses-sdk android:minSdkVersion="9" android:targetSdkVersion="15" />
    <supports-screens
        android:smallScreens="false"
        android:normalScreens="true"
        android:largeScreens="true"
        android:anyDensity="false"
    />
    <application>
        <uses-library android:name="com.google.android.maps"/>    
    </application>
</manifest>

  • I can't speak to the date picker, but I have not noticed that the UI in general looks so different from other native apps under android 4.0.

    As for newer features, I think that the Appcelerator team has probably been stretched a bit thin over the past 12-18 months with things like Titanium Studio, their cloud services, mobile web support, etc. If anything, I wish they'd address bugs/api holes in the current code rather than continuing to reach out for new platforms. But I can certainly see why they haven't implemented an Action Bar. I can't speak for them, but I think they're trying to cover the most commonly used components so you can build cross-platform apps that have more or less the same functionality on various platforms. If you want to build a bad-ass, cutting-edge android app that utilizes everything in the android UI toolkit, this isn't the right tool for you.

    The author of that blog post is not really looking deep enough at the product to make those generalizations.

    I'm sure there are a lot of bad attempts at cross-platform apps with Titanium Mobile. I would attribute that to a number of problems:

    • lack of platform parity (there are still many parts of the API that don't work on Android, but do work on iOS; it's quite buggy and poorly documented in that regard)

    • a bit of an "iphone first" mentality within the Appcelerator organization; as an outsider, I'm only guessing, but in my estimation, the docs and examples have had an iOS bias. That's probably a holdover from years past, when that was probably justified. Today, though, anybody who underestimates the importance of android vs. iOS is making a big mistake.

    • devs who build an entire app for iOS because they either think "iphone first" or the simulator is easier to use, and then try to port the whole thing over to android, resulting in a myriad of bugs and issues they'd never considered, like building interfaces for different screen sizes.

    I have written native Android apps – let me tell you that most of the problems people face in writing Ti Mobile apps for android are still there if you go native – you can't get away from the fact that there are different devices with different sized screens; it makes android development more complex than iOS development. I don't think a lot of Ti Mobile devs are prepared for that, or properly consider it in their app design/development.
