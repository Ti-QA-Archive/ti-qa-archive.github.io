    • Hi Ivan,

      I think that's a fair criticism. Where we as a company have been attacked, it usually commands more of a response from us. While it's a human response (to respond when attacked), it's not fair to people who are active contributors. In this sense, we are rewarding the wrong kind of behavior, which is something I will have to consider in how we build out and manage our community support and communications going forward.

      However, it's not a question of us being willing to help one person and not willing to help another. Our desire is to help everyone, but with a community of 200,000+ developers it would be difficult to do this without neglecting other areas of need. When dealing in a resource-constrained environment, the squeaky wheel tends to get the grease, which isn't fair for the reasons stated above.

      How we try to support developers (in Q&A and beyond) comes down to the following flow:

      • Do we have any threads in progress with community members? If so, follow those first. We try and make an impact with individual developers on individual issues as much as we can, and try and follow through on interactions from Q&A, IRC, e-mail, Twitter, Facebook, blog comments, etc. before moving on to new ones.
      • In Q&A, has the question received any answers or comments yet? If not, we try and take a look to at least get them started in the right direction. Comments to tease out clarifying information or direct answers.
      • Is there a question that's getting voted up, or one that seems to be generating a lot of discussion? This is an indication that developers care about the given question, and we try and weigh in if we can.
      • Unanswered questions

      While it may not be optimal, that's what we do. But with a community as large as ours, unfortunately our own individual impact does not travel as far as we would like. We don't have an ulterior motive or shadow agenda with how we answer questions, though - our genuine desire is to help as many people as we possibly can. You bring up a good point though - maybe there needs to be another bullet in this process to account for "good behavior" and not just "bad behavior" (discussion inducing questions). That's something we can try and bring into our process.

      I hope that gives some visibility into how we do community support. One mechanism we try and provide is to allow developers to reach out to us at community at appcelerator dot com to help nudge us toward issues they're seeing. It's not perfect, but again, our emphasis is on helping as many people as possible.

    • Point taken - we're currently pouring the vast majority of our engineering resources into Android dev to close this gap. They've always been resourced equally from a platform standpoint, but in many ways the Android platform requires more resources to develop for. This is being reflected in our own investments in engineering.

      To be quite clear, there are three things we hear loud and clear from the community that we need to work on:

      • Overall Platform Stability and Performance
      • API parity between iOS and Android (where appropriate)
      • Documentation

      These areas are receiving major investment across the board right now, and you'll all see major deliverables soon as we reorganize our wiki documentation, roll out normalized layout APIs and more parity work in 1.8, and implement V8 as our JavaScript engine for Android. We're also moving fast and decisively to staff up quickly on the platform and QA side - exciting updates there soon.

      We are listening, and we are our own harshest critics. We think Titanium is a great platform today, but can be (and must be) even better.

      Thanks, and reach out to us directly with any feedback (community at appcelerator dot com)
