    • This is due to Android having many variation and aspect ratios that often the best you can achieve is close when attempting to resolve splash screens or background images.

      This is where image scaling and device screen popularity within each density comes into play.

      Often people resort to using dot 9 images for splash screens - these allow you to define the content area of an image and then specify which portions of the image can be stretched and which cannot - this often leaves patches of solid colours - which whilst not ideal - at least does not stretch or compress the content area of the image.

      You are experiencing what we have all gone through when developing for Android - fragmentation, Android lovers hate the word, iOS lovers use it to berate Android lovers. A smart group of realists simply understand it to be fact and it is both the reason Android is successful and a pain to develop for in equal measures.

      Take a look at this report into the Android platform and the fragmentation, specifically the screen sizes to see what you are up against.

      The best thing to do is pick the most common resolutions, throw in a bit of dot 9 layout to the designs and test as many brand leading devices as you can get your hands on.

      The other thing that sometimes comes into play is density misreporting, sometimes the device will tell you it is a density but in reality the density should be something else. This is one of the many fun things about software development.

      Keep in mind if it was; easy, made sense and always worked - then we would not be the specialists we are - or in other words; we mad people trying to make mad things less maddening.

    • I would not try and create two different images for each density, if you do this - when will it ever end beyond creating over a hundred variations.

      Many of my Android apps have used the following sizes;

      • ldpi; 240x320
      • mdpi; 320x480
      • hdpi; 480x720
      • xghpi; 640x960

      If .9 files were used then each of those measurements are increased by two to allow for the single pixel edge that denotes the stretchable portion.

      • ldpi; 242x322
      • mdpi; 322x482
      • hdpi; 482x722
      • xghpi; 642x962

      Folder names are important here, for standard images in a classic app;

      • {appname}/resources/android/images/res-ldpi
      • {appname}/resources/android/images/res-mdpi
      • {appname}/resources/android/images/res-lhpi
      • {appname}/resources/android/images/res-xhdpi

      For standard images in an alloy app;

      • {appname}/app/assets/android/images/res-ldpi
      • {appname}/app/assets/android/images/res-mdpi
      • {appname}/app/assets/android/images/res-lhpi
      • {appname}/app/assets/android/images/res-xhdpi

      Tip: Alloy assets compile down to the same location that classic apps start off as

      For .9 images (classic or Alloy)

      • {appname}/platform/android/res/drawable-ldpi
      • {appname}/platform/android/res/drawable-mdpi
      • {appname}/platform/android/res/drawable-hdpi
      • {appname}/platform/android/res/drawable-xhdpi

      Whilst you can have .9 splash screens - I do in many apps, the support is not complete in current versions. However Ti SDK v3.2 looks to have full support for them without having to use to "hack" of platform folders. You can read more about it here;

      • https://jira.appcelerator.org/browse/TIMOB-10801
